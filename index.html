What trick are we missing in tackling obesity?

Obesity was historically considered an individual's health problem owing to poor diet and little exercise. Today there is growing evidence around its impact on society and the economy. Among the problems it's causing is its staggering cost for the NHS, posing as an opportunity cost of funds to spend on other healthcare.



How can we stop NHS costs attributable to obesity reaching these predictions?

The aim of this project is to interrogate the relationship obesity has with other variables, in the process, testing the strength of the evidence base some existing suggested policies rely on, and looking to see if there are any surprising correlations that we might want to pay closer attention to when evaluating how best to go about solving this socioeconomic problem.

What is obesity correlated with?

Reducing obesity to a problem caused by physical inactivity and eating too many calorific foods is clearly unhelpful, when these two factors are determined by so many variables. It is well known that income deprivation is correlated with weight, where leading a healthy lifestyle is expensive, so I wanted to focus on looking at other variables in the hope of exposing some lesser known about trends. There is a plethora of health-related data available from the government's website to interrogate the relationship between obesity and other variables, but looking through these one by one would take forever. Furthermore, some of the variables, despite existing in the database they use, are not published on the page. I discovered this after using an API in Python to scrape all data available at local authority level (360 indicators). After cleaning it, I calculated the correlations between obesity and each variable.

Do these correlations support the case for policies that have been suggested to tackle unhealthy habits?

By itself, the chart above unfortunately contains a lot of data that is too niche to be useful in devising new policies, particularly in the instances that being overweight is a by-product of another health complication or vice versa (e.g. hypertension), and not least forgetting that correlation is not causation! However, the process of producing this chart was useful because it taught me the code necessary to clean data so that I can compare obesity rates with other data out there and apply the results to evaluate why certain policies might or might not be effective.
For example, The government has looked into introducing policies regulating the growth of fast food outlets. The chart below hints they might not be very useful.
Although there is a positive correlation between outlet density and weight, the relationship is very weak, as shown by the poor fit of the regression line and low R squared value indicating that only 6% of the variation in obesity rates can be explained by how many outlets there are in an area. While there could be multiple benefits associated with implementing policies to limit the number of outlets in an area, it may not be a worthwhile endeavour to directly address obesity relative to other policies: arguably, it makes sense that this policy research paper has not taken further or updated since 2014.
